Integrating Model-based Prior with Reinforcement Learning for Bipedal Locomotion on Challenging Terrains

Shilong Sun1,2, Chiyao Li1, Haodong Huang1, Zida Zhao1, Dachuan Liu1, Qingbin Gao1, Yuanpeng Wang1, Wenfu Xu1,2
1Harbin Institute of Technology(Shenzhen), 2Guangdong Provincial Key Laboratory of Intelligent Morphing Mechanisms and Adaptive Robotics
Paper Video Code

IMPRL: Methods and Results.

Abstract

Model-free reinforcement learning (RL) methods rely on step-by-step trial and error and feedback to optimize policies, requiring a large amount of training time and computational resources. These methods often exhibit unstable gaits when transferred to physical robots. We propose a control framework that combines the reference signals generated by the LIP model with RL signals through a weighted fusion. This combination introduces prior information from the simplified model's gait and leverages the stability advantages of model-based methods. Meanwhile, the RL component retains the flexibility and adaptability of the control strategy. It is worth noting that our training consists of two phases: in the first phase, we introduce noise disturbance, thrust disturbance, and domain randomization; in the second phase, we remove these disturbances and implement curriculum learning for terrain to enhance the robot's terrain adaptability. Finally, simulation and real-world deployment results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed framework. In forward and turning control at different speeds, our method achieves stable gait control and precise speed tracking, and performs excellently on various challenging terrains. Moreover, the framework exhibits strong generalization capabilities, allowing it to be applied to a humanoid robot of other size without the need to re-adjust the reward terms and their weights.

Training Pipeline

The control signals are generated by two components: the model-based component and the RL policy component.

The model-based component , based on the 3D LIP model, outputs the reference action signals.

The RL component utilizes an actor-ritic architecture, where the actor network generates the RL action signals.
